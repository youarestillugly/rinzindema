Reflections on My Time at Sherubtse

I spent ten and a half years of my prime life at Sherubtse, then a public school, studying from class three to twelve. Sherubtse was a second home for me, spending nine and a half months every year there. The teachers, in particular the fathers, mothers, brothers, and sisters not only taught me subjects but they nurtured me. They helped define my personal constitution. I am what I am largely because of them and I shall always remain indebted to them!

Thank God that I passed late Father Coffee’s dictation on “Ugly Duckling” (though the foreign accent, which I was hearing for the first time, posed serious listening challenge), which took me to Sherubtse, I am proud to be a Sherubtsean!

Those were the days of innocence, simplicity, freedom (without worry), harmony, trust, and care, devoid of sensitivities, where late Father Mackey decided my age and what I should study. Other fond memories largely surround around truancy from games and sneaking into locked dormitory and rebellious behavior such as stealing Choeki Khorlo’s math homework book from the staff room to copy the answer to a question that we could not solve (Ugen Tshomo was the partner in crime) and poor Khorlo being beaten for no fault of his, plundering Aum Tashi Wangmo’s apple orchard, which was just below the girl’s hostel and her grandmother appearing in the morning assembly the next day with my new pair of slippers that was stuck in the mud as we tried to escape her fury. Less revealed the better! Maybe that’s the reason why I was chosen to work in the Anti-corruption Commission.

Like any other “educational” factory that mindlessly churns out products to suit the job market, is not what I want to envision of Sherubtse, the “Peak of Learning”. I want to envision my alma mater as the “Peak of GNH Learning” that nurtures GNH citizens, who uphold and internalize traditional wisdom and culture, who understand interdependence of life, care for others not just human beings but ecology as a whole, who are not lured by the powerful force of materialism, in essence who understand the quintessence of the eightfold noble path of the enlightened one. We must want to be different otherwise we are in the same rut of production of “robots” (no offense to anyone, only as a matter of speech).
